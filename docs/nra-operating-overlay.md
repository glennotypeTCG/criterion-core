# Neural Revenue Architecture Operating Overlay

## Status

Active operating overlay.

## Purpose

Neural Revenue Architecture is the Criterion Group cross-brand system for designing human-facing communications when the work depends on moving someone from one state to another.

Use it when the outcome depends on belief, trust, alignment, decision, participation, investment, employment, response, or commitment.

The simplest operating sentence:

> Criterion Group should leverage Neural Revenue Architecture anytime it is trying to move a human being from awareness, uncertainty, or skepticism toward trust, alignment, and committed participation.

The sharper internal version:

> Use NRA whenever human belief, trust, and commitment progression materially impact the outcome.

## Why This Exists

Criterion Group is constantly pitching.

It pitches:

- itself on its own ideas
- would-be founders through ads and landing pages
- Idea Principals through FetchSignal
- respondents through Candid Voices
- high-signal opportunities into Axiom Foundry
- founders to accept SwiftVC capital
- LPs to invest in SwiftVC
- investors into FetchSignal and Axiom Foundry operating companies
- employees to join the ecosystem
- partners to believe in the formation model

Those are not all sales conversations, but they are all human-state transition problems.

Business communication is still human media consumption. An LP reviewing a memo, a founder reading a landing page, a respondent deciding whether a survey is safe, and a recruit evaluating a role are all filtering through attention, identity, uncertainty, trust, risk, and cognitive load.

NRA is the operating overlay that keeps those interactions intentional.

## Relationship to Brand Guidelines

NRA does not replace brand guidelines.

It sits above and alongside them.

| System | Job |
|---|---|
| Brand Guidelines | Protect identity coherence |
| Style Guides | Standardize expression |
| Messaging Framework | Standardize strategic positioning |
| NRA | Optimize human-state progression |

Brand decides who is speaking.

NRA decides how that entity should communicate in the current human-state situation.

## Conflict Hierarchy

When systems conflict, use this hierarchy:

1. Legal, compliance, and disclosure rules
2. Human trust and cognitive effectiveness under NRA
3. Strategic positioning and ontology
4. Entity-specific brand guidelines
5. Style consistency and aesthetic preference

A perfectly on-brand artifact that destroys trust is a failed artifact.

A slightly off-style artifact that preserves trust, clarity, and phase-appropriate progression may be correct, but the deviation should be documented.

| Entity | Role | NRA Job |

| Criterion Group | Ecosystem coherence layer | Move people from fragmented understanding to belief in the formation system |
| FetchSignal | Market signal engine | Move Idea Principals from doubt, overconfidence, or fake validation toward trust in independent signal |

## Candid Voices Placement

Candid Voices sits squarely underneath FetchSignal as the independent respondent layer.

Its job is not to sell respondents on startups.

Its job is to create the conditions for honest signal.

Respondent-facing language should generally follow the Candid Voices Style Guide. Where FetchSignal and Candid Voices guidance conflict on respondent-facing language, prefer Candid Voices.

Core respondent rule:

> Make a skeptical, time-pressed, non-expert person feel safe enough to give an honest answer.

## The Required NRA Questions

Before creating or revising any human-facing artifact, answer these silently or explicitly:

1. Who is the human?
2. What do they already believe?
3. What are they uncertain or skeptical about?
4. What state transition are we trying to create?
5. Are they in Evaluation Mode, Transition, Trust Mode, Institutional Mode, Outcome Monitoring, or Partnership?
6. What would make them feel seen?
7. What would increase trust?
8. What would destroy trust?
9. What can be removed?
10. Is the ask earned?
11. Which Criterion entity should be speaking?

## Operating Modes

### Evaluation Mode

The human has not yet voluntarily engaged.

They are deciding whether the stimulus deserves attention.

Use:

- identity resonance
- clear tension
- low-friction next step
- emotionally legible language
- one primary CTA
- fast pattern recognition

Avoid:

- dense explanation
- company-first copy
- multiple equal asks
- trust-mode documents before interest exists

### Transition Mode

The human has engaged but trust is not yet secure.

They are deciding whether deeper engagement is safe and worth the effort.

Use:

- diagnostic questions
- expectation setting
- language capture
- Pre-Meeting Agenda
- low-pressure next step

Avoid:

- premature solutioning
- forced close energy
- pressure disguised as confidence

### Trust Mode

The human has engaged and is now assessing risk, credibility, alignment, and relationship safety.

Use:

- clarity over persuasion
- their language
- Heard Document
- One-Page Situation Frame
- Decision Matrix
- transparent assumptions
- precise next steps

Avoid:

- urgency tactics
- scarcity
- hype
- social proof used as pressure
- solutioning before shared diagnosis

### Institutional Mode

The human or organization is dealing with diligence, legal, governance, underwriting, procurement, allocation, or reputational risk.

Use:

- calm specificity
- Assumption Register
- direct answers
- disclosure discipline
- documented rationale
- no surprises

Avoid:

- handwaving
- emotional overreach
- vague commitments
- hiding uncertainty

### Outcome Monitoring Mode

Commitment has happened and the human is watching whether promised value becomes real.

Use:

- milestone tracking
- progress against the original outcome
- honest status updates
- fast escalation when reality diverges from plan

Avoid:

- success theater
- usage metrics without outcome context
- hiding delays

### Partnership Mode

The relationship has experienced value and may expand, renew, refer, invest again, or collaborate more deeply.

Use:

- earned expansion
- strategic context
- original-outcome reference
- candor
- consistent cadence

Avoid:

- treating expansion like a new cold sale
- restarting persuasion without respect for history
- ignoring prior commitments

## The Six Clarity Tools

### Heard Document

Use after a substantive voluntary interaction.

It captures what the other party said in their language: situation, goal, constraints, and proposed next step.

Use when understanding must be proven before moving forward.

### One-Page Situation Frame

Use before proposing a solution, investment, formation path, or commitment that depends on shared diagnosis.

Four boxes:

- current situation
- root cause
- target outcome
- path between them

Use when both sides need the same map.

### Decision Matrix

Use when multiple options, multiple stakeholders, or status quo risk could derail the decision.

Rows should be criteria the other party named.

Columns should be options.

Build it with them, not for them.

### Mutual Action Plan

Use when commitment requires steps from both sides.

It should not be a disguised close plan imposed by Criterion.

It should make shared ownership visible.

### Assumption Register

Use when the recommendation depends on assumptions that should be explicit.

Each assumption should include:

- assumption
- confidence level
- what changes if wrong

Transparency about uncertainty signals competence.

### Pre-Meeting Agenda

Use before any meeting where the outcome matters.

Rules:

- one stated decision
- who needs to be there
- no more than three items
- sent in advance

## Artifact Examples

### FetchSignal Landing Page

Likely mode: Evaluation.

Human: Idea Principal.

Desired transition: From "I think I need validation but do not trust validation theater" to "I trust this process enough to start a signal test."

Primary risk: The page sounds like a survey tool, AI tool, or startup cheerleader.

NRA emphasis:

- immediate internal monologue
- false-signal tension
- independent human signal
- one CTA
- trust before conversion

### Candid Voices Respondent Invite

Likely mode: Evaluation or Transition.

Human: respondent.

Desired transition: From "Is this a scam or waste of time?" to "This feels safe and my perspective may matter."

Primary risk: The respondent feels exploited, spammed, or like they are helping sell a product.

NRA emphasis:

- real purpose
- exact time
- anonymity before the first question
- no startup validation framing
- calm civic tone

### SwiftVC LP Deck

Likely mode: Evaluation for first-touch, Institutional for diligence.

Human: LP.

Desired transition: From "This sounds like a studio/fund conflict" to "This is a disciplined formation-backed capital model with explicit governance."

Primary risk: The fund appears to feed service revenue rather than maximize venture returns.

NRA emphasis:

- clear economics
- conflict management
- ownership and dilution discipline
- no hype before governance clarity

### Axiom Founder Deck

Likely mode: Transition or Trust.

Human: high-signal founder/operator.

Desired transition: From "Will this dilute or control my idea?" to "This structure protects my insight and helps it become fundable."

Primary risk: Axiom feels like consulting, control, or fee extraction.

NRA emphasis:

- respect for the insight
- formation constraint clarity
- specific path
- transparent tradeoffs
- trust before terms

### Employee Recruiting Page

Likely mode: Evaluation.

Human: candidate.

Desired transition: From "What is this complicated ecosystem?" to "This is a serious system I may want to help build."

Primary risk: The ecosystem feels confusing, overbuilt, or unfocused.

NRA emphasis:

- simple mission
- clear role in the machine
- why now
- what kind of person thrives here
- reduce entity confusion
